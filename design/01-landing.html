II.

How a certificate is impressed.

A three-act rite: an institution attests, the chain witnesses, a student holds. Each act is recorded in ink that cannot be rewritten.

Act the First

The Institution attests.

A pre-approved institution opens the ledger, fills in the student's name, course, and institution, and uploads the PDF. A single signature of MetaMask impresses the certificate upon the chain.

issueCertificate(student, …)
Act the Second

The Chain witnesses.

A cryptographic hash is cast from four fields — name, course, institution, IPFS link — and stored beside the token id. Any tampering, however small, produces a different hash. The forgery is instantly legible.

keccak256(abi.encodePacked(…))
Act the Third

The Student holds.

The certificate is minted as a soulbound token to the student's wallet. It cannot be sold, traded, or transferred. It is theirs, alone, for as long as the chain endures.

_update(…) reverts on transfer

IV.

On the question, “why a chain?”

For as long as there have been diplomas, there have been counterfeits. Every solution the world has offered — watermarks, embossed seals, holographic foil, the notarised stamp — has been defeated in turn by cheaper printers. The trouble, quite simply, is that paper is a medium one can copy. A hash is not.

Édiproof does not ask you to trust the institution, nor the registrar, nor the paper in your hand. It asks you only to trust mathematics: if the four fields you enter produce the same 32-byte fingerprint that sits upon the chain, the certificate is authentic. If they do not, it is not. There is no middle ground, and no authority to bribe.
Editor's margin We considered, briefly, storing the certificate PDF itself on the blockchain. But the chain is a poor library — it is expensive and ill-suited to carrying anything heavier than a number. So we store the PDF on IPFS, and the hash of everything on-chain. The two together constitute the full proof.

— The Editors
